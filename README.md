# Project Overview

Employee attrition is one of the most important Human Resource (HR) metrics because excessive turnover increases recruitment costs, disrupts operations, and affects organizational productivity.

This project analyzes HR employee data using Microsoft Excel to identify attrition patterns across departments, job roles, age groups, education levels, and employee demographics.

The dashboard enables HR managers and business leaders to quickly understand workforce trends and make informed decisions that improve employee retention.

---

# Project Objectives

The primary objectives of this project are to:

- Calculate the overall employee attrition rate.
- Identify departments with the highest turnover.
- Analyze attrition across different age groups.
- Evaluate employee exits by education level.
- Examine attrition by job role.
- Analyze employee distribution by gender.
- Measure average employee job satisfaction.
- Build an interactive HR dashboard for executive decision-making.
- Generate actionable recommendations for improving employee retention.

---

# Business Problem

Employee turnover is expensive.

Every employee resignation results in recruitment costs, onboarding expenses, productivity loss, and additional pressure on existing employees.

The HR department wanted answers to the following business questions:

- What is the organization's attrition rate?
- Which departments experience the highest employee turnover?
- Which employees are most likely to leave?
- Which job roles require immediate retention strategies?
- Does education level influence employee attrition?
- What employee groups should HR prioritize?

The objective was to convert HR data into meaningful insights that support strategic workforce planning.

# Key Insights

### 1. Overall employee attrition remains relatively high.

Out of **1,470 employees**, **237 employees left**, resulting in an attrition rate of **16.12%**.

---

### 2. R&D experiences the highest employee turnover.

The R&D department accounts for **133 employee exits**, significantly more than Sales (**92**) and HR (**12**).

---

### 3. Employees aged 25–34 are leaving most frequently.

The **25–34 age group** records **112 resignations**, making it the highest-risk employee segment.

---

### 4. Bachelor's Degree holders have the highest attrition.

Employees with a **Bachelor's Degree (99)** account for the highest number of departures.

---

### 5. Laboratory Technicians and Sales Executives experience the highest turnover.

Critical operational roles—including **Laboratory Technicians (62)**, **Sales Executives (57)**, and **Research Scientists (47)**—have the greatest employee turnover.

# Business Recommendations

### Improve Employee Retention Strategy

Conduct employee engagement surveys, stay interviews, and exit interviews to better understand why employees leave.

### Focus on High-Turnover Departments

Investigate workload, leadership quality, career growth opportunities, compensation, and work-life balance in the **R&D** and **Sales** departments.

### Strengthen Career Development

Introduce mentorship programs, structured career progression, leadership development, and continuous learning opportunities for employees aged **25–34**.

### Support Employee Growth

Offer tuition reimbursement, certifications, and internal promotion opportunities—particularly for employees holding Bachelor's Degrees.

### Prioritize High-Risk Job Roles

Review compensation, recognition, workload, staffing levels, and career development for:

- Laboratory Technicians
- Sales Executives
- Research Scientists
